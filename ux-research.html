02 User Interviews

30-minute semi-structured interviews conducted before participants used the product. Questions explored existing workflows, pain points, and expectations.

🎓
Nour Khalil — Content Researcher
Interview duration: 28 min · Remote via Zoom
Walk me through how you currently organize YouTube content for your clients.
Nour So basically I have a master Google Sheet that I've been building for like two years now. I open YouTube, go through the channel, and manually copy video titles and descriptions into a column, then in the next column I type the category myself. For some channels it's obvious — like if it's a cooking channel everything is Lifestyle or Food. But some tech channels post gaming content, some news channels do opinion pieces… so I end up second-guessing myself a lot. It probably takes me four to six hours to fully process one big channel.
"The categorization itself isn't hard — it's the volume. If I could get a first pass done automatically I'd just be reviewing, not doing from scratch."
What would make you trust an AI categorization result?
Nour Honestly, if I can see why it categorized something a certain way, even just a word or two, that would help a lot. But if I can export everything and skim through it in a spreadsheet I'd catch mistakes anyway. The big thing for me is accuracy on edge cases — like a video that's part tutorial, part vlog. I'd want to know it picked one and not just threw it into "Other" because it was confused.
How many channels would you process in a typical week?
Nour Varies. Could be one big one with 500 videos, or five smaller channels for a campaign audit. Peak is maybe eight channels in a week when we're doing competitive analysis for a pitch.
📊
Karim Mostafa — Marketing Analyst
Interview duration: 25 min · In-person
What does your current YouTube reporting workflow look like?
Karim I use a mix of Social Blade for basic stats and then manual digging for content type. My manager keeps asking "what percentage of their content is educational vs. promotional" and I have no reliable way to answer that fast. I end up estimating, honestly. I pick 20 videos at random, categorize them, and extrapolate. Not exactly scientific.
"If I could drop three URLs into a tool and get a pie chart 10 minutes later, that would genuinely change how I run my Monday reports."
What's the most important output for you — raw data or visual summaries?
Karim For the reports I share? Definitely visuals. Bar charts, percentages, the kind of thing I can screenshot and paste into a slide. But I also need the underlying numbers in case someone asks a follow-up. So both, really, but the visual comes first.
How do you feel about background processing — waiting for a job to finish?
Karim I'm fine with it as long as I know it's actually working. If there's no progress indicator I'll assume it broke. I've been burned by that before — submitted something, closed the tab, came back to nothing because it silently failed. So just tell me it's running, tell me when it's done.
🎮
Youssef Adel — Gaming Creator
Interview duration: 22 min · Remote via Google Meet
Have you ever tried to analyze a YouTube channel's content before?
Youssef Not really. I know some analytics exist in YouTube Studio but they don't tell me what type of content — just views and stuff. I mostly just eyeball it. Like I'll go to a competitor's channel and scroll through and mentally note "okay they post mostly walkthroughs, rarely do reaction videos." But I'm not writing anything down.
What would a useful result look like to you?
Youssef Something simple. Like, "this channel is 70% Gaming, 20% Entertainment, 10% Other." And then maybe I can click into the gaming pile and see which videos. I don't need all the nerdy stats — just the big picture with the ability to dig in if something surprises me.
"I want it to feel like I'm getting a cheat sheet on someone's channel, not reading a data report."
What would make you stop using a tool like this?
Youssef If it takes too long, or if the categories are obviously wrong. Like if it puts a Minecraft video in "Education" just because he's teaching you how to build something — that would make me not trust it at all. One wrong category and I'd second-guess everything.
🧑‍💻
Salma Ibrahim — ML Student
Interview duration: 35 min · Remote via Discord
You built something similar yourself — what did you find hardest?
Salma Getting the categories right on ambiguous content was the nightmare. Like "Technology" versus "Education" for a coding tutorial — technically both. And "Entertainment" is almost a catch-all if you're not careful. My model kept dumping borderline videos into Entertainment because that category had the most training examples. Class imbalance basically wrecked precision for smaller categories like Business and Sports.
What would you look for first when reviewing someone else's categorization tool?
Salma Honestly? I'd try to break it. I'd feed it videos that I know are ambiguous and see where it lands. I'd want to know: does it always fall back to "Other" when unsure, or does it make a confident wrong call? Both are bad but in different ways. I'd also look at whether "Uncategorized" is being used as a cop-out for anything the model isn't sure about.
"The interesting metric isn't how many it categorized correctly — it's how it handles the ones in the grey area."
Would you find it useful to see confidence scores on each video?
Salma Absolutely. Even a simple low/medium/high or a percentage would let me trust the results differently. I'd filter to "low confidence" first and review those. Right now if there's no score I either trust everything or trust nothing, and neither is the right approach.

05 Key Findings & Opportunities

Synthesized from interviews, sessions, and surveys. Findings are categorized as pain points, gains, insights, or opportunities.

Pain Point
Silent background processing causes anxiety
4 out of 5 participants expressed uncertainty after submitting a channel URL. The "Processing started" toast message is the only feedback — after that the screen goes quiet with no indication of progress or estimated time.
"I thought it broke. Nothing changed on screen so I just sat there not knowing if it was working." — Omar
Observed in: Sessions 1, 2, 3, 5 · Survey rating avg: 3.4/5 for ease of use
Gain
CSV export is immediately recognized as a workflow win
Both professional users (Nour and Karim) independently called out the Export CSV feature as the highest-value action in the app. It maps directly to their existing spreadsheet workflows without requiring any reformatting.
"This might replace my whole manual sheet." — Nour
Observed in: Session 1 · Survey: all 4 professional users rated this feature 5/5
Pain Point
Optional URL fields read as required to first-time users
Two participants (Youssef and Omar) hesitated on the channel input form, unsure whether they needed to fill all three URL fields. The subtitle says "up to 3" but the visual weight of three identical empty inputs implies all three are expected.
"They look like I'm supposed to fill them all. I only tried with one because I decided to just risk it." — Youssef
Observed in: Sessions 3, 5 · Quick fix: add "Optional" placeholder to fields 2 and 3
Insight
Reports page is the clearest screen — across all skill levels
Every participant navigated directly to Reports at some point without prompting and could answer "what's the dominant category?" within 10 seconds. The metric cards + distribution bars are the most universally legible layout in the app.
"I got my answer in five seconds. I didn't have to think about it." — Omar
Survey: Reports clarity rated 4.5/5 average · Fastest task completion across all sessions
Opportunity
Per-channel filter on Reports would unlock competitive comparison
Karim's core use case — comparing two competitor channels — is blocked by the lack of a channel filter in Reports. Currently all processed channels are merged into one aggregate view. A simple channel dropdown would unlock the competitive analysis workflow for analysts.
"I can't easily compare two channels against each other. That's the whole point for my use case." — Karim
Requested by: Karim (explicitly) · Implied by: Youssef (wanted "cheat sheet per channel")
Opportunity
Confidence scores would increase trust for technical users
Salma and Nour both independently identified accuracy trust as a blocker for full adoption. Salma wants low/high confidence flags to prioritize review. Nour would use them to spot-check before delivering to clients. A lightweight confidence indicator (e.g., a colored dot) would serve both needs.
"I can't tell the difference between a confident correct answer and a lucky guess." — Salma
Requested by: Salma (explicitly), Nour (implied) · Aligns with the ML model's probability output
Pain Point
Videos vs. Results sidebar distinction is unclear
Omar discovered during the session that "Videos" (flat list) and "Categorized Results" (grouped by category) serve different use cases, but couldn't predict the difference from the sidebar labels alone. Both sound like they show video content.
"What's different between Videos and Results? I honestly couldn't tell from the labels." — Omar
Observed in: Session 5 · Suggested fix: rename "Videos" → "All Videos (flat)" or add subtitle labels in sidebar
Opportunity
Inline category override would serve power users and close the feedback loop
Salma specifically requested the ability to manually correct a miscategorized video inline, noting it would also be valuable as training data feedback. Even without retraining integration, a simple override that updates the DB category would dramatically increase data quality for professional use cases.
"Let me fix it right there and maybe even feed that back as training data eventually." — Salma
Requested by: Salma (explicitly) · Would benefit: Nour (quality control before client delivery)
